mechanisms of meaning

This week’s reading covered the artistic journeys of master diagram makers Arakawa and Gins through the book “The Mechanism of Meaning.” The intro covers how Arakawa first got into the art scene here in the United States, creating a network of famous artists including musician and filmmaker Yoko Ono, composer John Milton Cage, and pop artist Andy Warhol through his work in Japan and New York. In New York City, he connected with Marcel Duchamp, learning and becoming more associated with diagrammatic art (through Duchamp’s French-educated perspective) instead of the traditional landscape and portrait studies artists underwent. I found this part interesting, especially since the author chose to dub diagramming as an “artistic visual language”, explicitly merging the worlds of language and art, where those are typically exclusive, one used to express the other and one used to describe the other. At this point, I decided to actually look up excerpts of the actual text of “The Mechanism of Meaning,” and was greeted with images that to me were bizarre and incomprehensible. A page on localization and transference provided me with sentences claiming “if possible look at this” with “if possible look at that” right under it, complete with a drawing of block letters writing out “THAT”, left me bewildered. What could this mean? The format of the pages reminded me slightly of optical illusions; short bits of text attached to diagrams meant to fool your mind. I returned to the original article to read more, and realized that I had done exactly as Arakawa and Gins intended; I questioned the image, the content of the few pages I was able to find in the book, and I observed the pages of this book from within my own subjective reality. What I had considered nonsense mere minutes ago I now looked at through a different lens, understanding that this artwork is not based solely in traditional art styles solely for the viewer’s pleasure (in fact, it is barely based in that at all) but rather meant to probe into the reader’s worldview while simultaneously introducing their own. This is not just artistic visual language, which is something I would personally label numerous pieces of artwork under, but philosophical artistic questioning, intended to get the viewer to think and respond to a vague prompt. The author of this article compares Arakawa’s and Gins’ work to “playful physics,” and I agree in that it is intellectually intensive and takes the viewer to their analytical limits.
